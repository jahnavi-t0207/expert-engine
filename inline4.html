The Anatomy of an Inline 4

Scroll down to explore how 4 cylinders in a single straight line work together to produce reliable power — the world's most popular engine layout, found in everything from economy cars to hot hatches.

Scroll down to dissect
🖱️
01 / 10

Intake Manifold

Visual: The structure sitting directly on top of the cylinder head, running the full length of all 4 cylinders.

Function: Channels fresh air from the throttle body into each of the 4 intake ports equally. On a well-tuned I4, runner length is optimised for a specific rev range — longer runners build torque low down, shorter ones boost power at high RPM.

02 / 10

Fuel Rail & Injectors

Visual: A slender pipe running parallel along the intake manifold, with 4 injectors plugging directly into the ports.

Function: Supplies pressurized fuel to each injector, which sprays a fine mist of petrol into the intake port at exactly the right moment before the intake valve opens. Modern I4s use direct injection — spraying straight into the cylinder at very high pressure.

03 / 10

Cylinder Head

Visual: A single long rectangular casting bolted flat across the top of the block — one head covers all 4 cylinders in a row.

Function: Houses the entire valvetrain (camshafts, valves, springs, rockers) and forms the roof of every combustion chamber. The head gasket below it seals combustion pressures that can exceed 50 bar.

04 / 10

Camshaft (DOHC)

Visual: Two long shafts with egg-shaped lobes running side by side the full length of the head — one for intake valves, one for exhaust.

Function: Driven by the crankshaft at half its speed, each cam lobe pushes its valve open at exactly the right point in the piston's 4-stroke cycle. Variable valve timing (e.g., VTEC, VANOS) adjusts this timing on the fly for more power or better efficiency.

05 / 10

Spark Plugs

Visual: 4 plugs threaded vertically into the head — one per cylinder, centred above the piston crown for the most efficient flame propagation.

Function: Each plug fires a 20,000–40,000 volt spark at the precise moment the piston reaches the top of the compression stroke. This ignites the compressed air-fuel charge, causing a controlled explosion that drives the piston down.

06 / 10

Cylinder Block

Visual: A compact, upright rectangular block — all 4 cylinder bores are arranged in a single straight vertical row.

Function: The engine's backbone. Cast from iron or aluminium, it contains the 4 precision-honed bores, coolant water jackets surrounding each cylinder to control temperature, and main oil galleries feeding every moving part.

07 / 10

Pistons & Connecting Rods

Visual: 4 cylindrical pistons moving up and down in the bores, each attached via a conrod to the crank below.

Function: Each piston goes through 4 strokes: Intake (down), Compression (up), Power (down), Exhaust (up). The I4 fires in the order 1-3-4-2, with a power stroke every 180° of crankshaft rotation, giving a steady but characteristic thrum.

08 / 10

Crankshaft

Visual: A single straight crankshaft at the base of the block, with 4 offset crank pins at 180° intervals.

Function: Converts all 4 pistons' linear motion into rotation. Because pistons 1 & 4 always move together (and 2 & 3 together), a secondary imbalance at twice engine speed is created — this is why most performance I4s include a Lanchester balance shaft system to eliminate vibration.

09 / 10

Timing Belt / Chain

Visual: Runs up one end of the engine, linking the crankshaft sprocket at the bottom to the two cam sprockets at the top.

Function: Maintains the critical 2:1 ratio between the crankshaft and camshafts. If this synchronisation is lost — from a snapped belt or jumped chain — pistons will collide with open valves causing catastrophic and instant engine failure.

10 / 10

Oil Pan & Pump

Visual: The shallow pressed-steel or cast aluminium pan bolted under the entire block, forming the engine's base.

Function: Holds 4–5 litres of engine oil. The crankshaft-driven oil pump sucks from here and pushes oil at 40–60 PSI through galleries to every bearing, cam lobe, and cylinder wall. Without this, the engine seizes within seconds.

End of Tour

You've just traced the complete 4-stroke combustion cycle of an Inline 4 — from fresh air through the intake manifold, to the crankshaft spinning your wheels, to the oil keeping every part alive.
